Process Reward Model · Reasoning · Step-Level Supervision

Let's Verify Step by Step

Instead of only rewarding a correct final answer, reward each intermediate reasoning step. A Process Reward Model (PRM) catches errors early in the chain — dramatically improving mathematical reasoning in LLMs.

Let's Verify Step by Step
Hunter Lightman, Vineet Kosaraju, Yura Burda, Harrison Edwards, Bowen Baker, Teddy Lee, Jan Leike, John Schulman, Ilya Sutskever, Karl Cobbe  ·  arXiv 2023  ·  OpenAI
1 · The Core Problem — Outcome Rewards Are Not Enough

🧩 What Goes Wrong with Final-Answer Rewards

  • Standard RL for reasoning rewards the model only if the final answer is correct — this is called an Outcome Reward Model (ORM).
  • A chain of reasoning can contain many steps. A single early mistake — even a subtle one — propagates forward and corrupts the final answer.
  • With ORM: the entire trajectory gets zero reward even if 9 out of 10 steps were correct. The model gets no signal about which step failed.
  • This creates sparse, noisy gradients — making it very hard for the model to learn to reason reliably.
Step 1
Let the two numbers be x and y. We're told x + y = 30 and x · y = 221.

Step 2
Using the identity (x − y)² = (x + y)² − 4xy = 900 − 884 = 16, so x − y = 4.

Solving x + y = 30 and x − y = 4: adding gives 2x = 34, so x = 16.

Under ORM: this entire trajectory gets reward 0 because the final answer is wrong. But steps 1 and 2 were correct — the error happened in step 3. A process reward model knows exactly where to intervene.
2 · The Key Idea — Process Reward Models

💡 Reward Every Step, Not Just the Final Answer

  • A Process Reward Model (PRM) assigns a reward score to each individual reasoning step in the solution.
  • Human annotators label each step as positive (correct), negative (incorrect), or neutral (skip).
  • The PRM is trained to predict these per-step labels, learning to identify where reasoning goes wrong — not just whether the final answer is right.
+ Positive — step is correct − Negative — step contains an error ~ Neutral — step not assessed
The PRM score for a full solution can be defined as the minimum step score (the weakest link) or the product of all step scores — both capturing the idea that a chain is only as strong as its worst step.
3 · ORM vs PRM — Full Comparison

🔀 Outcome vs Process Supervision

🎯 Outcome Reward Model (ORM)
  • Scores only the final answer
  • Reward = 1 if correct, 0 otherwise
  • No signal about which step failed
  • Easy to label — just check the answer
  • Reward hacking: model may guess or use illegal shortcuts
  • Cheaper to collect data for
  • Weak signal for long reasoning chains
🔬 Process Reward Model (PRM)
  • Scores each reasoning step individually
  • Reward per step: positive / negative / neutral
  • Pinpoints exactly where reasoning breaks down
  • Expensive to label — requires math expertise
  • Harder to hack — each step must be correct
  • Data-intensive but much stronger signal
  • Enables step-level search and correction

4 · PRM800K — The Dataset

📦 Building Ground-Truth Step-Level Labels

  • The paper introduces PRM800K — a dataset of 800,000 step-level human labels on math solutions generated by GPT-4.
  • Problems sourced from MATH — a challenging benchmark of competition-level math problems (algebra, number theory, geometry, etc.).
  • Human annotators — with strong math backgrounds — label each step in a solution as positive, negative, or neutral.
  • Multiple solutions per problem were generated and labeled, providing diverse reasoning paths and error types.

5 · Training the PRM

🏋️ How the Reward Model Is Trained

  • The PRM is initialized from a pretrained language model (GPT-4 base in this work).
  • A special step delimiter token is inserted after each reasoning step in the solution.
  • The model learns to predict, at each delimiter, whether the step so far is correct — a binary classification at each step boundary.
PRM Training Objective
L(ψ) = − Σi=1N [ yi · log pψ(correct | s1:i, x) + (1−yi) · log(1 − pψ(correct | s1:i, x)) ]
x — problem prompt
s1:i — all reasoning steps from step 1 to step i
yi — human label for step i (1 = correct, 0 = incorrect)
pψ — PRM's predicted probability that step i is correct
Binary cross-entropy at each step delimiter token.
PRM Score for a Full Solution
PRM(x, s1:N) = mini pψ(correct | s1:i, x)
The solution score is the minimum step probability — the weakest link in the chain.
Alternative: product of all step probabilities (more sensitive to all errors simultaneously).
6 · Using the PRM at Inference Time

🔍 Selecting the Best Solution via PRM Scoring

  • At inference, the generator model produces N candidate solutions to a problem (via temperature sampling).
  • The PRM scores each solution using its minimum (or product) step score.
  • The solution with the highest PRM score is selected as the final output — this is called Best-of-N with PRM.
  • Unlike ORM best-of-N (which only checks final answers), PRM best-of-N can distinguish between two solutions that both reach the correct answer but via different quality reasoning chains.
🎲
Best-of-N (ORM)

Generate N solutions. Score each by final answer correctness. Pick the majority vote or highest ORM score.

🔬
Best-of-N (PRM)

Generate N solutions. Score each by the minimum step score. Pick the solution with the highest PRM score.

🌲
Beam Search (PRM)

Generate step-by-step. At each step, use PRM to prune low-scoring paths. Keeps only the K best partial solutions.

PRM beam search is the most powerful use case — it prunes bad reasoning paths early, preventing error propagation instead of just filtering complete solutions post-hoc.
7 · Full Pipeline

⚙️ Step-by-Step

  1. Sample a problem from the MATH dataset. Use the generator model (GPT-4) to produce multiple candidate step-by-step solutions.
  2. Present solutions to human annotators (math-trained). For each solution, annotators label every step as positive (+), negative (−), or neutral (~). Store in PRM800K dataset.
  3. Insert a special delimiter token after each step. Fine-tune the PRM on PRM800K: at each delimiter, predict whether the preceding step is correct (binary cross-entropy loss).
  4. At evaluation time, sample N solutions for a test problem. Compute PRM score for each solution as the minimum step probability across all steps.
  5. Select the solution with the highest PRM score. Compare against: (a) majority voting, (b) ORM best-of-N, (c) random selection.
  6. Evaluate the chosen solution against the ground-truth answer on the MATH benchmark.

9 · Why PRM Beats ORM — The Deep Intuition

🎯 What ORM Gets Fooled By

  • Lucky correct answers: A solution can reach the right answer via flawed reasoning (two errors cancel). ORM rewards this; PRM penalizes the bad steps.
  • Answer guessing: A model can guess the numeric answer correctly without reasoning at all. ORM is blind to this; PRM requires each step to be justified.
  • Error propagation: ORM can't distinguish a solution that fails at step 2 vs step 9. PRM knows step 2 failed and can prune that path immediately in beam search.
PRM enforces faithfulness — it's not enough to get the right answer, the path to it must also be correct. This is particularly critical for scalable oversight: as problems get harder, humans can't check answers but can still check individual steps.
10 · Connection to Scalable Oversight

🔭 Why Process Supervision Matters for AI Safety

  • Scalable oversight is the problem of supervising AI systems that become smarter than their human evaluators.
  • For a very hard problem, a human may not be able to verify the final answer is correct — but may still be able to verify each reasoning step one at a time.
  • Process supervision provides a path to overseeing super-human AI: break down the task into verifiable steps, even when the whole is too complex to evaluate directly.
  • This connects to debate and amplification — other scalable oversight techniques where complex tasks are recursively decomposed.
The underlying philosophy: it is easier to verify than to generate. Even if a human can't solve a hard math problem, they can usually check whether a given algebraic manipulation is valid.
11 · Annotation Challenges

⚠️ Difficulties in Collecting Process Labels

  • Annotator expertise: Step-level math labels require annotators with strong mathematical background — far more specialized than the labelers in standard RLHF.
  • Ambiguous steps: Some steps are partially correct — they use a valid technique but with a minor arithmetic error. Labeling these requires judgment.
  • Step granularity: What constitutes one "step" is not always obvious. Too fine-grained = excessive annotations; too coarse = error localization suffers.
  • Cost: 800K labels required substantial human effort — significantly more expensive per label than outcome annotation.
  • Neutral labels: Many steps were labeled neutral (skipped), reducing the effective training signal.
12 · Pros & Cons of Process Supervision

⚖️ Strengths & Limitations

Strengths
  • Dense reward signal — faster, more stable learning
  • Precise error localization in reasoning
  • Enables powerful step-level beam search
  • More robust to reward hacking
  • Aligns with scalable oversight goals
  • PRM800K publicly released
Limitations
  • Expensive expert annotation required
  • Hard to define steps consistently
  • Only evaluated on math — may not generalize
  • Step granularity affects results significantly
  • Requires model outputs to be structured as discrete steps
13 · Impact & What Came Next

🚀 Legacy of Process Supervision

  • OpenAI o1 / o3 (2024) — "chain-of-thought reasoning models" trained with process-level RL. PRM-style step verification is central to their training pipeline.
  • DeepSeek-R1 (2025) — uses GRPO with per-step reward shaping inspired by process supervision. Achieves o1-level reasoning at a fraction of the cost.
  • Math-Shepherd (2024) — replaces human step labels with automated step verification using the model itself, scaling PRM annotation cheaply.
  • OmegaPRM / AutoPRM — use Monte Carlo Tree Search to automatically assign process rewards without human labels at all.
  • Critique-based RLVR — extends process supervision beyond math to coding, science, and multi-step tool use.
This paper established the foundational principle behind all modern "reasoning models": training the model to produce verifiable intermediate steps, not just correct final answers. Every reasoning-focused LLM from o1 onward builds on this idea.
