DeepSeek-R1: Incentivizing Reasoning Capability in LLMs via Reinforcement Learning

The first open-source model to match OpenAI o1 on reasoning benchmarks — trained with pure RL (no SFT cold start in R1-Zero), showing that chain-of-thought reasoning can emerge entirely from reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards.

DeepSeek-R1: Incentivizing Reasoning Capability in LLMs via Reinforcement Learning
DeepSeek-AI: Daya Guo, Dejian Yang, Haowei Zhang, Junxiao Song, Ruoyu Zhang, Runxin Xu, Qihao Zhu, Shirong Ma, Peiyi Wang, Xiao Bi, et al.  ·  arXiv Jan 2025  ·  DeepSeek-AI
1 · Overview & Key Contributions

🎯 What This Paper Does

  • Trains DeepSeek-R1-Zero — applies GRPO directly to a base LLM without any supervised fine-tuning. Reasoning behaviors emerge purely from RL with verifiable rewards.
  • Trains DeepSeek-R1 — a refined 4-stage pipeline (cold-start SFT → reasoning RL → rejection-sampling SFT → alignment RL) that achieves o1-level performance.
  • Demonstrates the "aha moment" — a critical emergent behavior where the model spontaneously learns to re-examine its own steps, backtrack, and self-correct without being taught to do so.
  • Open-sources the model weights and distills reasoning into smaller models (1.5B – 70B), making frontier reasoning accessible to the community.

2 · Two Models — R1-Zero vs R1

🔀 What's the Difference?

⚡ DeepSeek-R1-Zero
  • GRPO applied directly to the base model
  • No SFT cold start — zero human demonstrations
  • Proves reasoning emerges from RL alone
  • Achieves strong reasoning but has issues: language mixing, poor readability, occasional repetition
  • A research proof-of-concept
🌟 DeepSeek-R1 (Full)
  • 4-stage training pipeline with cold-start SFT
  • Fixes R1-Zero's language mixing and readability
  • Adds general-domain capabilities (not just math/code)
  • Production-quality reasoning model
  • Matches o1-preview/o1-mini on most benchmarks
3 · DeepSeek-R1-Zero — Reasoning from Pure RL

🔬 What Happens When You Apply GRPO to a Raw Base Model

  • Starting point: DeepSeek-V3-Base — a 671B MoE model pretrained on general text (no reasoning-specific SFT).
  • Apply GRPO with only two rewards: accuracy (is the answer correct?) and format (is the response inside <think>…</think> tags?).
  • No demonstrations of chain-of-thought. No human-written reasoning examples. Just: answer questions, get rewarded if correct.
  • Over training, the model spontaneously develops longer thinking traces, self-verification, and backtracking.
This is the paper's most remarkable finding: reasoning is not taught, it emerges. Given the right incentive (be correct), a large language model discovers that thinking step-by-step helps it be correct.
4 · The "Aha Moment" — Emergent Self-Reflection

✨ The Most Striking Emergent Behavior

  • During training, the model begins to produce responses where it re-reads its own reasoning, catches errors, and restarts its approach — without ever being shown this behavior.
  • The paper calls this an "aha moment" — a point where the model's behavior discretely shifts from linear reasoning to recursive self-correction.
  • The behavior is genuinely emergent — it is not present in early training and gradually appears as the model discovers that rechecking improves reward.
✨ Example: R1-Zero Thinking Trace (emergent)
<think> Let me solve this step by step. The equation is 2x + 5 = 13.

Subtracting 5 from both sides: 2x = 8, so x = 4.

Wait, let me re-examine this. I should verify: 2(4) + 5 = 8 + 5 = 13. ✓

Actually, I realize I should double-check whether the problem is asking for x or something else... Yes, x = 4 is the final answer. </think>
The "wait, let me re-examine" pattern was never in the training data. It emerged because the model learned that pausing to verify its work increased its chance of getting reward. This is analogous to a student discovering the habit of checking their work.
5 · Reward Design for R1-Zero

🏆 Two Simple Rewards — No Reward Model Needed

Accuracy
Rule-based verifier checks if the final answer matches ground truth. For math: symbolic equivalence (1/2 = 0.5). For code: execution against test cases. Binary: 1 (correct) or 0 (wrong).
primary
Format
Reward for placing reasoning inside <think>…</think> tags and the answer inside <answer>…</answer> tags. Encourages structured, readable outputs.
secondary
No Language Penalty
R1-Zero does NOT penalize language mixing. This is intentional — the model is free to reason in whatever language helps. This causes readability issues but is not constrained in Zero.
absent
Crucially, there is no neural reward model — all rewards are computed by a deterministic program. This completely eliminates reward hacking against a learned reward model.
6 · R1-Zero's Limitations

⚠️ What Pure RL Gets Wrong

  • Language mixing — R1-Zero freely mixes Chinese and English mid-reasoning. Technically fine for accuracy, but unreadable for users who only speak one language.
  • Poor readability — reasoning traces are often verbose, repetitive, and hard to follow. The model optimizes for correctness, not clarity.
  • Repetition loops — occasionally enters degenerate loops of repeated sentences before self-correcting or failing.
  • No general-domain capability — the RL training is focused on math and code. The model loses general instruction-following ability developed during pretraining.
These issues motivated the full DeepSeek-R1 pipeline — a 4-stage process that fixes these problems while preserving the emergent reasoning capability that R1-Zero discovered.
7 · DeepSeek-R1 — Four-Stage Training Pipeline

🏗️ From Base Model to Production Reasoner

①
Cold Start · SFT
Cold Start Supervised Fine-Tuning

Fine-tune DeepSeek-V3-Base on a small set (~thousands) of high-quality long chain-of-thought examples — including human-written reasoning traces and outputs from R1-Zero. Establishes the <think> format and readable reasoning style before RL begins.

②
Reasoning RL · GRPO
Reasoning-Focused Reinforcement Learning

Apply GRPO with accuracy + format rewards on math, science, and code problems. Now that the model has seen good reasoning examples (Stage 1), the RL can refine it further without starting from scratch. Language consistency reward added here.

③
Rejection Sampling · SFT
Rejection Sampling + General SFT

Sample many outputs from Stage 2 model. Keep only the correct ones (rejection sampling). Fine-tune on this curated set plus general instruction-following data (writing, QA, summarization). Recovers general-domain capabilities lost during RL.

④
Alignment · GRPO
Final Alignment Reinforcement Learning

A final GRPO pass on the full task mix — reasoning + general tasks. Uses a mix of rule-based rewards (math/code) and a learned reward model (for general helpfulness/safety). Produces the final aligned DeepSeek-R1 model.

8 · Why the Cold Start Is Critical

❄️ Seeding RL with Good Reasoning Format

  • R1-Zero proves RL can discover reasoning from scratch — but it's slow and produces messy outputs.
  • The cold-start SFT seeds the policy with the right format — structured, readable <think> traces — before RL begins.
  • With this seed, the RL in Stage 2 converges faster and to better local optima — the model already "knows what good reasoning looks like".
  • Cold-start data includes: long CoT examples curated from the web, outputs from R1-Zero filtered for quality, and human-written multi-step reasoning demonstrations.
Analogy: R1-Zero is a student left alone to figure out how to study. DeepSeek-R1 gives the student one good tutoring session first (cold-start SFT), then leaves them to practice independently (GRPO). The outcome is far better.
9 · GRPO in the R1 Context

⚡ The RL Algorithm Unchanged from DeepSeekMath

  • DeepSeek-R1 uses the same GRPO algorithm introduced in DeepSeekMath — the core algorithm is not changed.
  • For each question, sample G outputs from the old policy. Normalize rewards within the group. Update with clipped PPO objective + KL penalty vs reference model.
GRPO Objective (same as DeepSeekMath)

ri(θ) = πθ(oi|q) / πθ_old(oi|q) — probability ratio
πref — frozen SFT model from Stage 1 (cold-start checkpoint)
Full Reward in Stage 2 (three components)
r(o, q) = racc(o, q) + rfmt(o) + rlang(o)
racc — accuracy reward: 1 if correct, 0 if wrong (rule-based)
rfmt — format reward: positive for well-structured <think>/<answer> tags
rlang — language consistency: penalizes mixing Chinese/English mid-reasoning
10 · Rejection Sampling Fine-Tuning

🔄 Curating a High-Quality Training Set from the RL Model

  • After Stage 2 GRPO, the model is a strong reasoner but weak on general tasks.
  • Rejection sampling: sample many (e.g. 8–16) solutions per reasoning problem. Keep only those that are correct and well-formatted. Discard the rest.
  • This produces a curated set of high-quality reasoning traces that can be used for SFT.
  • Combine with a large set of general instruction-following data (writing, QA, summarization, code) to restore general-domain capability.
  • Fine-tune the Stage 2 model on this combined dataset → produces a model that is both a strong reasoner and a good general assistant.
Rejection sampling is like self-play data curation — the model generates its own training data, which is then filtered for quality. The RL model improves the SFT data, which then improves the final model.
11 · Distillation — Spreading Reasoning to Small Models

🌱 Teaching Reasoning to 1.5B–70B Models

  • The long chain-of-thought traces generated by DeepSeek-R1 are used as SFT training data for smaller models.
  • Small models (1.5B–70B) are fine-tuned on ~800K R1 reasoning traces — no RL needed for the distilled models.
  • Surprisingly, this SFT distillation alone transfers much of R1's reasoning ability to tiny models — far better than applying GRPO directly to small models.
  • Models distilled from: Qwen2.5 (1.5B, 7B, 14B, 32B) and Llama-3 (8B, 70B).

13 · Thinking Tokens — Compute at Inference Time

💭 More Thinking = Better Answers

  • R1's <think> traces can be very long — thousands of tokens — for hard problems. This is intentional.
  • The model trades inference-time compute (more tokens generated) for better accuracy. This is the "test-time compute" paradigm introduced by o1.
  • For easy problems, the model produces short thinking traces. For hard problems, it self-explores extensively before committing to an answer.
  • This is fundamentally different from training-time scaling — the model dynamically allocates more compute per problem based on difficulty.
Test-Time Compute Scaling
Performance ↑ as |thinking tokens| ↑ for hard problems
Unlike model size scaling (more parameters), this scales inference compute.
The model learns when to think longer — short traces for easy problems, long for hard ones.
This is emergent: the model was only rewarded for correctness, not for thinking length.
14 · Pros & Cons

⚖️ Strengths & Limitations

Strengths
  • Fully open-source with released weights
  • Matches o1 at far lower training cost
  • Emergent reasoning — no process supervision needed
  • Distillation works surprisingly well for small models
  • Rule-based rewards — no reward hacking
  • Dynamic inference-time compute allocation
Limitations
  • Long thinking traces — very high inference cost
  • Still weaker on general reasoning vs coding/math
  • Language mixing in R1-Zero (fixed in R1)
  • Distilled models don't match R1 on hardest problems
  • Sensitive to the cold-start data quality
  • Training requires massive GPU clusters
15 · Full DeepSeek-R1 Training Algorithm

⚙️ Step-by-Step

  1. Start with DeepSeek-V3-Base (671B MoE, general pretrained LLM).
  2. Stage 1 — Cold Start SFT: Collect thousands of high-quality long CoT examples. Fine-tune the base model on these using standard SFT. This establishes the <think> format and readable reasoning style.
  3. Stage 2 — Reasoning RL: Apply GRPO to the Stage 1 model. Rewards: accuracy (rule-based), format, and language consistency. Train on math, code, and science problems with verifiable answers. Allow emergent behaviors (self-reflection, backtracking) to develop.
  4. Stage 3 — Rejection Sampling SFT: Sample 8–16 outputs from the Stage 2 model per problem. Filter for correct + well-formatted ones. Combine with general instruction-following data. SFT the Stage 2 model on this combined set to restore general capabilities.
  5. Stage 4 — Final Alignment RL: Apply a final GRPO pass with mixed rewards: rule-based for math/code, learned RM for general helpfulness/safety. Produce the final DeepSeek-R1 model.
  6. Distillation: Generate ~800K long CoT reasoning traces from R1. SFT smaller models (1.5B–70B) on these traces to produce DeepSeek-R1-Distill variants.
16 · Legacy & What Came Next

🚀 Impact on the Field

  • Open-source o1-level reasoning — R1's open release democratized frontier reasoning models. Any researcher can now fine-tune R1-distill variants.
  • DAPO (Apr 2025) — "Decoupled Clip and Dynamic sAmpling" extends GRPO with token-level advantages and clip asymmetry to improve training stability.
  • Dr. GRPO / RLOO — variants that further improve group sampling efficiency and reduce variance.
  • Qwen-QwQ, Kimi-k1.5, Phi-4-reasoning — subsequent open reasoning models from other labs adopting the same paradigm.
  • S1 / Sky-T1 — showed that even 1K curated long-CoT SFT examples can produce strong reasoning models — pushing the "budget forcing" approach further.
  • OpenAI o3 — OpenAI's next generation, hypothesized to use similar RL-based reasoning with verifiable rewards and longer test-time compute.
DeepSeek-R1 proved three things simultaneously: reasoning emerges from RL alone, open-source can match closed-source frontiers, and distillation is more efficient than training small models from scratch with RL.
