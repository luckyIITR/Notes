RLHF · Reward Learning · Human Feedback

Deep RL from Human Preferences

A framework for training RL agents using human preference judgements instead of hand-crafted reward functions — the origin paper of modern RLHF.

Deep Reinforcement Learning from Human Preferences
Paul Christiano, Jan Leike, Tom Brown, Miljan Martic, Shane Legg, Dario Amodei  ·  NeurIPS 2017  ·  OpenAI & DeepMind
1 · The Core Problem

🧩 Why Reward Functions Are Hard

  • RL requires a reward function that precisely encodes what we want the agent to do. In practice, this is extremely difficult.
  • Hand-crafted rewards are brittle — small mis-specifications lead to unintended behaviors (reward hacking).
  • For complex tasks like "do a backflip gracefully" or "write a helpful response", it is nearly impossible to write a formula that captures human intent.
  • The ideal signal is what humans actually prefer — but querying humans for a scalar reward at every timestep is impractical (thousands of steps per episode).
Key insight: Humans are much better at comparing two behaviors ("which clip is better?") than at assigning a precise numerical reward to a single behavior. This paper exploits that asymmetry.
2 · The Big Idea

💡 Learn the Reward, Then Optimize It

  • Instead of specifying a reward, learn a reward model r̂(s, a; ψ) from human preference labels.
  • A human is shown pairs of short behavior clips and asked: "Which one do you prefer?"
  • These preference labels train the reward model via supervised learning.
  • A standard RL algorithm (e.g. PPO, A2C) then optimizes a policy using the predicted rewards from the learned model.
  • The two processes — reward learning and policy optimization — run simultaneously and interactively.
3 · Full Pipeline

🔄 Three Interleaved Components

① Policy
RL Agent (πθ)
Acts in the environment. Optimized using predicted rewards from r̂. Generates trajectory segments for human review.
↕
② Selector
Clip Selector
Samples pairs of short trajectory segments (σ¹, σ²) from the agent's recent experience and sends them to the human.
↕
③ Human
Human Annotator
Watches both clips and labels which segment is preferred (or marks them equal / incomparable). Labels stored in preference database 𝒟.
↕
④ Reward Model
Reward Model (r̂ψ)
Trained on 𝒟 to predict which segment a human would prefer. Outputs per-step reward estimates used by the RL agent.
The loop is asynchronous and online: as the agent improves, new clips are generated from its better behavior, which then refine the reward model further.
4 · Human Preference Queries

👁️ What the Human Sees

  • A trajectory segment σ = (s1, a1, s2, a2, …, sk) is a short clip of ~25 timesteps of the agent's behavior.
  • The human watches two clips side-by-side and picks the one that better achieves the intended goal.

  • The label is stored as a tuple (σ¹, σ², μ) where μ is a distribution over {1, 2, tie, incomparable}.
  • Only ~1,400 preference labels were needed to teach MuJoCo agents complex locomotion — far fewer than environment reward signals.
  • Labels can come from a human, a synthetic oracle, or a preference model (as in modern RLHF).
5 · Reward Model Training

🏋️ Learning r̂ from Preferences

  • The reward model r̂(s, a; ψ) is a neural network that assigns a scalar reward to each (s, a) pair.
  • Training objective: given a preference label (σ¹ ≻ σ²), maximize the probability that the model assigns higher cumulative reward to σ¹.
Preference Probability (Bradley-Terry Model)
P̂[σ¹ ≻ σ²] = exp(Σt r̂(s¹t, a¹t)) / ( exp(Σt r̂(s¹t, a¹t)) + exp(Σt r̂(s²t, a²t)) )
Models the segment with higher total predicted reward as the preferred one.
This is a softmax over summed rewards of the two segments.
Reward Model Loss (Cross-Entropy)
L(ψ) = − E(σ¹,σ²,μ)∈𝒟 [ μ(1)·log P̂[σ¹≻σ²] + μ(2)·log P̂[σ²≻σ¹] ]
μ(1) — label weight for σ¹ being preferred (0 or 1 for hard labels, 0.5 for ties)
𝒟 — database of all collected human preference labels
Standard cross-entropy loss, just like binary classification.
  • An ensemble of reward models is trained. Variance across the ensemble can be used to query the most informative clips — active learning.
  • The reward model is normalized (zero mean, unit variance) to prevent scale from dominating policy updates.
6 · Policy Optimization

🎯 Training the Agent with r̂

  • The RL agent receives r̂(st, at; ψ) as its reward signal instead of the environment's true reward.
  • Any standard on-policy algorithm can be used — the paper uses A2C (Advantage Actor-Critic).
  • The policy is optimized to maximize the cumulative predicted reward.
Policy Objective
J(θ) = Eπθ[ Σt γt · r̂(st, at; ψ) ]
Identical to standard RL objective — except the reward comes from the learned model, not the environment.
The agent never sees the true environment reward during training — only the predicted reward from the human-trained model. Yet it achieves competitive performance because r̂ learns to approximate what humans care about.
7 · Three Training Phases

⚙️ How Training Proceeds

Phase 1
Pre-training (optional)

Agent pre-trains briefly with true rewards (or random) to generate non-trivial clips. Ensures early human queries aren't completely uninformative.

Phase 2
Reward Model Bootstrap

~400 human labels collected on pre-training clips. Initial reward model r̂ trained. RL training begins using r̂ rewards.

Phase 3
Online Interleaved Loop

New clips generated from improving policy. ~900 more labels collected. r̂ is continuously retrained. Policy keeps optimizing against updated r̂.

8 · Full Algorithm

🔄 Step-by-Step

  1. Initialize policy πθ, reward model ensemble {r̂ψ}, and empty preference database 𝒟.
  2. Run policy in the environment for a warmup period (random or pre-trained) to collect initial trajectory segments.
  3. Sample pairs of segments (σ¹, σ²) and show them to a human annotator. Store label (σ¹, σ², μ) in 𝒟.
  4. Train reward model ensemble on 𝒟 using cross-entropy loss over Bradley-Terry preference probabilities.
  5. Normalize the reward model outputs (zero mean, unit variance over recent transitions).
  6. Run RL (A2C/PPO) with reward r̂(st, at) to improve policy πθ.
  7. Periodically select new trajectory pairs from the improved policy — prioritize pairs where ensemble members disagree most (active learning).
  8. Collect more human labels for these pairs. Add to 𝒟. Retrain reward model.
  9. Repeat steps 6–8 until policy converges.
9 · Active Learning — Smart Query Selection

🔍 Choosing Which Clips to Show

  • Human labeling time is expensive — we want each query to be as informative as possible.
  • The reward model ensemble (multiple independently trained models) provides a measure of uncertainty.
  • Clip pairs where ensemble members disagree most on which is better are the most informative to query.
  • This is a form of uncertainty sampling from active learning.
Query Selection Criterion
Select (σ¹, σ²) maximizing Vari[ P̂i[σ¹ ≻ σ²] ]
P̂i — preference probability predicted by reward model i in ensemble
High variance → models disagree → label is most informative

11 · Reward Hacking & Limitations

⚠️ Failure Modes

  • Reward model overfitting — if the reward model is wrong in a region the policy then exploits, behavior degrades. The agent optimizes for r̂, not the true goal.
  • Distribution shift — the reward model was trained on clips from an earlier, worse policy. As the policy improves, clips may go out-of-distribution.
  • Human inconsistency — labelers may be noisy, biased, or inconsistent, injecting errors into the reward model.
  • Clip length sensitivity — preferences on short 25-step clips may not reflect preferences over full episodes.
  • Scalability — in this paper, ~1,400 human labels for one task. This grows with task complexity.
These limitations motivated subsequent work: Constitutional AI (replacing human labels with AI feedback), DPO (bypassing the reward model entirely), and iterated reward modeling.
12 · Pros & Cons

⚖️ Strengths & Limitations

Strengths
  • No hand-crafted reward function needed
  • Very few labels required (~hundreds to thousands)
  • Works on tasks where reward is hard to specify
  • Active learning minimizes human burden
  • Framework is algorithm-agnostic (any RL algo)
Limitations
  • Reward model can be exploited (reward hacking)
  • Human labels are noisy and inconsistent
  • Distribution shift as policy improves
  • Still requires real human time
  • Hard to scale to very long-horizon tasks
13 · Modern RLHF — From Paper to ChatGPT

🚀 How This Paper Became the Foundation of LLM Alignment

2017
Christiano et al. — This Paper
Demonstrated RLHF on MuJoCo locomotion and Atari with a small number of human preference labels.
2019–2020
OpenAI — Fine-Tuning Language Models
Steerability paper applies RLHF to GPT-2 fine-tuning for summarization and instruction following.
2022
InstructGPT (Ouyang et al.)
Full 3-step RLHF pipeline (SFT → Reward Model → PPO) applied to GPT-3. Human labelers prefer InstructGPT outputs over GPT-3 175B despite being 100× smaller.
2022
ChatGPT
InstructGPT technique applied at scale. Same 3-step RLHF pipeline. Became the most-adopted AI product in history.
2023–present
DPO, GRPO, Constitutional AI
Alternatives and extensions: DPO eliminates the reward model, GRPO eliminates the critic, RLAIF replaces human labels with AI feedback.
14 · Modern RLHF Pipeline for LLMs

💬 Three-Step LLM Alignment Process

  1. Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT): Fine-tune the base LLM on high-quality human-written demonstrations of desired behavior. This creates the initial aligned model.
  2. Reward Model Training: Sample pairs of model outputs for the same prompt. Human labelers pick the preferred output. Train a reward model (RM) on these preference pairs using the same Bradley-Terry cross-entropy loss from the original paper.
  3. PPO Fine-Tuning: Further fine-tune the SFT model using PPO, with the RM providing reward signals. A KL penalty is added to prevent the policy from deviating too far from the SFT model: r = r̂(x,y) − β · KL[πRL ‖ πSFT].
The KL penalty against the SFT model is critical — without it, PPO rapidly collapses the policy to output degenerate text that maximally exploits the reward model (reward hacking).
