Scoping

How to pick the right machine learning project to work on — separating problems from solutions, running diligence on feasibility and value, and planning milestones and resources.

1 · Why Scoping Matters

🧭 Picking the Right Project

Picking the right project to work on is one of the rarest and most valuable skills in AI today. It's tempting to jump straight into the first project you get excited about — sometimes that works out — but if you or your team will spend weeks, months, or longer on something, it is well worth thinking through a few options and selecting the most promising one before putting in all that effort.

Consider an e-commerce retailer looking to increase sales. A quick brainstorm could produce dozens of ideas: a better product recommendation system, better search, fixing missing or incomplete catalog data, inventory management (how many shirts to buy, where to ship them), or price optimization.

Of all the ideas you could work on, some are 2×, 5×, or 10× more valuable than others. Being able to pick the most valuable project significantly increases the impact of your work.

Machine learning is a general-purpose tool — there is a lot you could do with it. The questions scoping answers are: what project(s) should we work on, what are the metrics for success, and what resources (data, time, people) are needed to execute.

2 · The Scoping Process

🔄 A Process for Choosing Projects

When speaking with a company for the first time about their AI projects, this is the end-to-end process to follow:

  1. Identify a business problem (not an AI problem) — brainstorm with a business or product owner.
  2. Brainstorm AI solutions for those problems — and accept that not every problem has one.
  3. Assess feasibility and value of the candidate solutions through diligence.
  4. Determine milestones for the chosen project.
  5. Budget for resources needed to execute.
Engage in divergent thinking first — brainstorm many possibilities — followed by convergent thinking, narrowing down to one or a small handful of the most promising projects.
3 · Separate Problem from Solution

🎯 Identify the Business Problem First

The first step is to get together with a business or product owner — often not an AI person — and brainstorm their business or application problems. At this stage you are trying to identify a business problem, not an AI problem. A useful framing: "I don't want to hear about your AI problems, I want to hear about your business problems" — and then it's your job to see if there's an AI solution. Sometimes there isn't, and that's fine.

Asked "what are the top three things you wish were working better?", an e-commerce retailer might say:

  • Increase conversions — more website visitors converting to a sale.
  • Reduce inventory — less stuff sitting around in the warehouse.
  • Increase margin — more profit per item sold.
Separating the identification of the problem from the identification of the solution leads to better solutions — a principle you'll also find in the writings on design thinking.

💡 One Problem, Many Solutions

Only after the business problems are clear do you brainstorm possible AI solutions. It's common for one problem to lead to multiple solution ideas:

Problem · Increase conversion
  • Improve website search result quality.
  • Better product recommendations from purchase history.
  • Redesign how products are displayed on a page.
  • Surface the most relevant product reviews.
Problem · Reduce inventory
  • Demand prediction to better estimate how many people buy.
  • A marketing campaign to drive sales of overstocked products.
Problem · Increase margin
  • Optimize merchandising — decide what is worth selling.
  • Recommend bundles (e.g. a camera with a protective case).

Problem identification is thinking through what you want to achieve; solution identification is thinking through how to achieve it.

4 · Diligence: Technical Feasibility

🔍 Can This Even Be Built?

Before starting, you assess a project for technical feasibility and value — a phase sometimes called diligence (a term borrowed from the legal field, meaning double-checking that something you hope is true really is true). Start with feasibility.

One quick gauge is an external benchmark — the research literature, publications, or the fact that another company or even a competitor has already built a similar search, recommendation, or inventory system. If someone else has done something similar, that's a sign your project may be feasible.

To complement or replace an external benchmark, use a two-by-two matrix over unstructured vs structured data and new vs existing systems. New means delivering a brand-new capability; existing means scoping an improvement to a system already doing the task.

🚦 HLP on Unstructured Data

Because people are very good at unstructured-data tasks, human level performance (HLP) benchmarks what might be doable. The key criterion: can a human, given the exact same data as the learning algorithm, perform the task?

Say a self-driving car must classify a traffic light as red, yellow, or green. Show a person an image from the car's camera and ask which lamp is illuminated. Sometimes it's clearly green; sometimes the image is so dark nobody can tell which lamp is lit. A human sitting in the car might have known — the human eye has superior contrast to most cameras — but the useful test is whether a person seeing only the camera image can do the task.

Making sure the human sees only the same data as the algorithm is critical. Many teams worked for a long time on a computer-vision system because a human physically inspecting the phone could find the defect — only to realize a human looking only at the image couldn't. The efficient fix is a better camera or lighting, not a better algorithm.

📊 Predictive Features on Structured Data

For structured-data problems, the key feasibility question is: do we have input features x that seem predictive of the target y?

Inputs x → Target yVerdict
Past purchases → future purchasesWorth a try — past purchases are predictive.
Weather → shopping mall foot trafficWorth a try — fewer people leave home when it rains.
DNA → heart diseaseIffy — genotype-to-phenotype is a very noisy mapping.
Social media chatter → future fashion demandIffy — predicting trends 6 months out is very hard.
A stock's history → its future priceNot doable with only price features, per the evidence.
A common way AI projects go poorly: the data simply isn't predictive, and the algorithm ends up doing barely better than random guessing. Checking for predictive features is an important diligence step.

📉 History of the Project

For an existing project, the previous rate of progress is a surprisingly good predictor of the future rate of progress. Take speech recognition, using HLP as an estimate of Bayes error (the irreducible error). Suppose error started at 10% in Q1 and fell each quarter after.

Simple model of progress
gap to HLP shrinks ~30% each quarter
gap = current error − HLP  ·  error decays exponentially toward HLP

If each quarter the team closes roughly 30% of the gap between current performance and HLP, the error curve decays exponentially toward HLP. Extrapolating this rate gives a reasonable sense of what's feasible for an existing project with a track record.

5 · Diligence: Value of a Project

💰 ML Metrics vs Business Metrics

Estimating a project's value isn't easy. In most businesses there are metrics that ML engineers optimize and metrics that product/business owners care about — and there's usually a gap between them. Take a speech recognition system for voice search:

  • Word-level accuracy — what fraction of words are transcribed correctly (close to what the algorithm optimizes).
  • Query-level accuracy — how often every word in a query is right.
  • Search result quality — does the user get a good answer?
  • User engagement — do users come back more often?
  • Revenue — the metric the business ultimately cares about.

The engineering team usually wants to work on the left end; the business leader wants promises on the right. The further right you go, the harder it is for an ML team to guarantee an outcome.

For a project to move forward, get the technical and business teams to agree on metrics both are comfortable with — the ML team stretching a little right, the business team stretching a little left. Compromise requires both sides to step slightly outside their comfort zone.

🧮 Fermi Estimates

Even very rough back-of-the-envelope calculations (also called Fermi estimates) help bridge ML metrics and business metrics. If word-level accuracy improves 1%, what does that do to query-level accuracy (maybe +0.7%), search result quality, user engagement, and revenue? A crude estimate is enough to connect the engineering work to business value.

⚖️ Ethical Considerations

Estimating value also means giving thought to ethics:

  • Is this project creating net positive societal value? If not, don't do it.
  • Is it reasonably fair and free from bias?
  • Have ethical or values-based concerns been openly aired and debated?

Values and ethics are highly domain-dependent — very different for loans, healthcare, or product recommendations. Look up ethical frameworks developed for your industry, and raise any concerns for debate within your team.

Some projects have a completely sound economic case but still shouldn't be built. It is worth killing a project that you don't believe genuinely helps people — focus your efforts on work that helps move humanity forward.
6 · Milestones and Resourcing

📋 Writing the Key Specifications

Once the problem, solution, feasibility, and value all look promising, the last steps are milestones and resourcing — writing out the project's key specifications:

  • ML metrics — accuracy, precision/recall, and sometimes fairness metrics.
  • Software metrics — latency, throughput, queries per second, given available compute.
  • Business metrics — e.g. estimated incremental revenue, if you can estimate it.
  • Resources — how much data, from which teams, personnel, cross-functional help, software integrations, and support needed from other teams.
  • Timeline — when you hope to hit certain milestones or deliverables.

🧪 When You Can't Specify Yet: Build a POC

If you're having a hard time writing some of these specifications, consider a benchmarking exercise against similar projects others have done, or build a proof of concept (POC) first to get a better sense of what accuracy, precision, latency, or throughput is feasible. Use what the POC reveals to more confidently scope the milestones and resources for a larger-scale execution.

This framework — scoping → data → modeling → deployment — closes the full cycle of a machine learning project, and helps you pick valuable, meaningful work before committing weeks or months to it.
