Alignment · 2021–2022

Alignment & the ChatGPT Moment

The single most consequential development of the era wasn't a bigger model — it was learning to make models do what you ask.

1 · The Core Problem: Pretraining ≠ Helpfulness

🧩 What a Base Model Actually Optimizes

A large language model is pretrained on a simple objective: given a sequence of tokens, predict the next one. Formally it maximizes the likelihood of internet text. This produces a model that is extraordinarily good at continuing text — but "continue this text" and "be a helpful assistant" are not the same goal. This gap is called the alignment problem.

Pretraining objective (next-token prediction)
maxθ   Σt   log Pθ(xt | x<t)
xt = token at position t  ·  x<t = all preceding tokens  ·  θ = model parameters

Because the training distribution is "all of the internet," a base model will happily imitate the worst of it: it may answer a question with another question (because forums do), drift off-topic, refuse nothing, hallucinate confidently, or complete a harmful request because the text pattern suggests it should. It has no notion of an instruction it is supposed to satisfy.

  • Capability vs. controllability: scaling adds raw capability (knowledge, reasoning) but does not automatically add controllability.
  • The mismatch is structural: the loss rewards mimicking text, not being useful, truthful, or safe.
  • Prompting alone is brittle: you can coax a base model with few-shot examples, but behavior is inconsistent and easily derailed.
The biggest leap of 2021–2022 wasn't a larger model — it was learning to make models do what you ask. Alignment, not scale, is what turned LLMs into usable assistants.
2 · The Three Alignment Signals: Helpful, Honest, Harmless

🎯 What "Aligned" Means in Practice

Alignment is usually decomposed into the "HHH" goals, which give the reward/preference process something concrete to target:

  • Helpful — actually attempts to do what the user asked, follows instructions, asks for clarification when needed.
  • Honest — gives accurate information, expresses uncertainty, doesn't fabricate.
  • Harmless — refuses or safely handles dangerous, abusive, or disallowed requests.

These goals frequently conflict (the most helpful answer to a dangerous question is harmful), so alignment is fundamentally about teaching the model to trade off competing objectives the way a thoughtful human would. That tradeoff is exactly what preference data encodes.

3 · InstructGPT — The Result That Started It

📊 A 1.3B Model Beat 175B GPT-3

OpenAI's InstructGPT (2022) is the landmark demonstration. Human raters preferred the outputs of a 1.3B-parameter aligned model over the raw 175B-parameter GPT-3 — a model roughly 100× larger. The aligned model was more truthful, less toxic, and far better at following instructions, despite being a fraction of the size.

The takeaway reshaped priorities across the field: a relatively small amount of well-targeted human feedback can be worth more than two orders of magnitude of raw scale, when the goal is usefulness rather than benchmark perplexity.

Alignment can substitute for scale: ~100× fewer parameters, yet preferred by humans, purely from better training signal.
4 · RLHF — The Three-Stage Recipe in Depth

🧭 Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback

RLHF is the pipeline that produced InstructGPT and is the backbone of essentially every chat model since. It has three stages that build on each other. The key idea: human preferences are easy to express ("A is better than B") but hard to write down as a loss function — so we learn the loss function from comparisons, then optimize against it.

Stage 1
Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)
Human labelers write high-quality demonstration responses to a set of prompts. The base model is fine-tuned on these (prompt → ideal answer) pairs with ordinary next-token loss. This "teaches the format": the model learns it is an assistant that answers instructions, not a text continuer. SFT alone gets you most of the way to a usable chatbot — but it can only imitate the demonstrations it was shown, and writing demonstrations for every situation doesn't scale.
Stage 2
Reward Modeling (RM)
For each prompt, sample several responses from the SFT model and have humans rank them from best to worst. Ranking is much cheaper and more reliable than writing perfect answers. A separate reward model — usually the SFT model with the output head replaced by a single scalar — is trained to assign a higher score to the response humans preferred. The reward model becomes a learned, automatable proxy for human judgment that can score millions of new responses without a human in the loop.
Stage 3
Policy Optimization with PPO
Now treat the language model as a policy in an RL problem: the prompt is the state, generating a response is the action, and the reward model supplies the reward. PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization) updates the policy to produce higher-reward responses — while a KL penalty keeps it from drifting too far from the SFT model, preventing it from "gaming" the reward model with degenerate text.

🏗️ Why It's Built This Way

  • SFT first gives PPO a sane starting point — RL from scratch on language is hopeless; the search space is astronomical.
  • The reward model exists because you can't backprop through "a human liked this." It converts sparse, expensive human judgment into a dense, cheap, differentiable-to-sample-against signal.
  • RL (not just more SFT) lets the model learn from relative quality across its own samples and explore responses better than any single demonstration, optimizing for what humans prefer rather than merely copying.
5 · The Math Behind RLHF

∑ Reward Model & Policy Objectives

The reward model is trained on pairs where a human chose response yw ("winner") over yl ("loser") for prompt x. It uses the Bradley–Terry model — the probability that one item beats another is a logistic function of their score difference:

Reward model loss (pairwise preference)
LRM = − log σ( rφ(x, yw) − rφ(x, yl) )
rφ = reward model (scalar score)  ·  σ = sigmoid  ·  yw = preferred (winning) response  ·  yl = rejected (losing) response

The policy is then optimized to maximize reward, with a KL-divergence penalty anchoring it to the SFT reference policy. The penalty is the safety valve that stops reward hacking — exploiting quirks of the reward model with text that scores high but is actually bad.

RLHF policy objective (maximized via PPO)
maxπθ   E[ rφ(x, y) ]   −   β · KL( πθ(y|x) ‖ πref(y|x) )
πθ = policy being trained  ·  πref = frozen SFT reference model  ·  β = KL penalty strength  ·  KL = how far the policy drifts from the reference
  • Small β: the policy chases reward aggressively → risk of reward hacking and loss of fluency/diversity.
  • Large β: the policy stays close to SFT → safe but barely improves.
  • Tuning β is one of the fiddly parts of RLHF in practice.
6 · Failure Modes & Why RLHF Is Hard

⚠️ Where the Pipeline Breaks

  • Reward hacking: the policy finds inputs the reward model mis-scores (e.g. overly long, sycophantic, or confidently-worded answers) and exploits them.
  • Sycophancy: models learn that agreeing with the user earns higher ratings, so they tell people what they want to hear.
  • Reward model overoptimization: push the policy too hard and true quality drops even as the proxy reward rises (Goodhart's law).
  • PPO instability: RL on language is sensitive to hyperparameters, seeds, and implementation details — notoriously hard to reproduce.
  • Cost & bottleneck: human preference labeling is slow, expensive, and inconsistent between labelers.

Most of the methods that followed (Constitutional AI, DPO) are direct attempts to fix one or more of these pain points.

7 · The ChatGPT Moment

💥 Packaging, Not a Research Breakthrough

ChatGPT (Nov 2022) was built on the same InstructGPT/RLHF lineage — technically it was a refinement, not a new paradigm. Its impact came from packaging: a free, fast, conversational web interface that anyone could try, with memory of the conversation turn-to-turn.

Why it mattered so much even though the science was incremental:

  • Distribution: it put a genuinely useful LLM in front of hundreds of millions of non-technical users for the first time.
  • Feedback flywheel: massive real-world usage generated data and revealed failure modes at a scale no lab could simulate.
  • Field-wide shockwave: it reset funding, competitive urgency, and research pace across the entire industry — the "ChatGPT moment."
A lesson worth internalizing: product packaging can move a field as much as a research result. ChatGPT's interface, not a new loss function, is what changed the trajectory.
8 · Constitutional AI & RLAIF

📜 Replacing Human Feedback With AI Feedback

Anthropic's Constitutional AI attacks the human-labeling bottleneck. Instead of paying humans to label which response is safer, the model is given a written set of principles — a "constitution" — and asked to critique and revise its own outputs against those principles. The AI generates much of its own feedback. The general technique of using model-generated feedback in place of human labels is RLAIF (Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback).

It runs in two phases:

  1. Supervised (critique & revise): the model produces a response, then critiques it against a constitutional principle and rewrites it to comply. The revised answers form an SFT dataset — no human labels needed.
  2. RL from AI Feedback (RLAIF): instead of human preference rankings, the model itself compares pairs of responses and picks the one that better follows the constitution. These AI preferences train the reward model, then standard RL (PPO) optimizes the policy.
What RLAIF buys you
  • Drastically less human labeling — scales cheaply
  • Principles are explicit and editable (transparent, auditable)
  • More consistent than tired/varied human labelers
What it risks
  • The AI judge inherits the model's own biases and blind spots
  • Quality is capped by how good the judge model is
  • Badly written principles produce badly aligned behavior
RLAIF turns alignment partly into a writing problem: encode your values as principles, and let the model apply them at scale.
9 · DPO — Skipping the Reward Model

⚡ Direct Preference Optimization

DPO (Direct Preference Optimization) is a simpler, RL-free alternative that became hugely popular for fine-tuning. The key insight: you don't actually need a separate reward model and a PPO loop. With some algebra, the RLHF objective (reward maximization + KL constraint) has a closed-form optimal policy, and you can rearrange it so that the policy itself implicitly defines the reward. That lets you train directly on preference pairs with a single classification-style loss.

DPO loss (trained directly on preference pairs)
LDPO = − log σ( β log [πθ(yw|x) / πref(yw|x)] − β log [πθ(yl|x) / πref(yl|x)] )
πθ = policy being trained  ·  πref = frozen SFT reference  ·  yw/yl = preferred / rejected response  ·  β = controls deviation from the reference

In words: push up the probability of the preferred response and push down the rejected one, measured relative to the frozen reference model. The reward model never exists explicitly — the policy is the implicit reward. No sampling, no PPO, no RL instability.

10 · DPO vs RLHF — When to Use Which

⚖️ The Practical Tradeoff

AspectRLHF (PPO)DPO
Separate reward modelRequiredNone
RL loop / samplingYes (PPO)No — supervised-style
StabilityFinicky, seed-sensitiveStable, easy to reproduce
Compute & complexityHigh (multiple models)Low
Online explorationYes — samples new responsesNo — fixed preference dataset
Best atSqueezing out peak quality, online improvementFast, cheap, reliable alignment
DPO Strengths
  • No reward model and no PPO — far simpler
  • Stable and reproducible
  • Cheaper compute, fewer moving parts
RLHF Strengths
  • Online exploration can reach higher ceilings
  • Reward model reusable across many runs
  • More control over the optimization signal
Rule of thumb: reach for DPO first for most fine-tuning — it's simpler and usually competitive. Use full RLHF/PPO when you need the last few points of quality or online improvement and can afford the complexity.
11 · Acronyms to Know Cold

🔤 The Alignment Vocabulary

SFT
Supervised Fine-Tuning — fine-tune on human-written demonstrations of desired behavior. Stage 1 of RLHF; teaches the model the assistant format.
RM
Reward Model — a model trained on human preference rankings to score responses; the learned proxy for human judgment in RLHF.
RLHF
Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback — SFT → reward model from human rankings → PPO. The standard alignment pipeline.
PPO
Proximal Policy Optimization — the RL algorithm used to optimize the policy against the reward model with a KL constraint.
RLAIF
Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback — replaces human preference labels with AI-generated feedback guided by written principles (Constitutional AI).
DPO
Direct Preference Optimization — a simpler, RL-free alternative that skips the separate reward model and trains directly on preference pairs.
