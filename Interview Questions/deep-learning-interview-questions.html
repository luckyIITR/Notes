Interview Questions · Deep Learning

Deep Learning Interview Questions & Answers

Thirty questions covering the deep-learning stack — backprop, activations, CNNs, RNNs/LSTMs, attention and Transformers, optimization, and the training tricks that make modern networks actually work.

1 · Fundamentals

🔁 How Networks Learn

1 What is backpropagation and how does it work?
Backpropagation computes gradients of the loss with respect to each weight using the chain rule, propagating errors backward from output to input layers. It has two phases: forward pass (compute predictions and loss) and backward pass (compute gradients layer by layer). These gradients are then used by an optimizer to update weights.
2 Why do we need activation functions, and what happens without them?
Activation functions introduce non-linearity, enabling networks to learn complex patterns. Without them, stacking layers collapses into a single linear transformation (a composition of linear functions is still linear), so the network couldn't model non-linear relationships regardless of depth.
3 Compare ReLU, sigmoid, and tanh.
Sigmoid (0 to 1) saturates and causes vanishing gradients; outputs aren't zero-centered. Tanh (−1 to 1) is zero-centered but still saturates. ReLU (max(0, x)) avoids saturation for positive inputs, is computationally cheap, and speeds convergence, but suffers "dying ReLU" (neurons stuck at 0). Variants like Leaky ReLU and GELU address this.
4 What is the dying ReLU problem and how do you fix it?
When a neuron's input is consistently negative, ReLU outputs 0 and its gradient is 0, so it stops updating permanently. Fixes: Leaky ReLU (small negative slope), Parametric ReLU, ELU, GELU, lower learning rates, and proper initialization.
20 Explain the difference between epoch, batch, and iteration.
An epoch is one full pass through the training dataset. A batch is the subset of samples processed before one weight update. An iteration is one weight update (one batch). iterations per epoch = dataset size / batch size.
29 What is the difference between parameters and hyperparameters?
Parameters (weights, biases) are learned from data during training. Hyperparameters (learning rate, batch size, number of layers, dropout rate) are set before training and control the learning process, typically tuned via validation.
2 · Initialization, Normalization & Stability

⚖️ Keeping Training Stable

5 Explain weight initialization strategies (Xavier vs He).
Poor initialization causes vanishing/exploding activations. Xavier (Glorot) initialization scales weights by variance 1/n to keep signal variance stable, suited for tanh/sigmoid. He initialization uses 2/n, designed for ReLU since it halves activations. Both prevent gradients from shrinking or blowing up early in training.
6 How does batch normalization work and what are its benefits?
It normalizes layer inputs per mini-batch to zero mean/unit variance, then applies learnable scale (γ) and shift (β). Benefits: faster training, higher learning rates, reduced internal covariate shift, mild regularization, and less sensitivity to initialization. At inference, it uses running population statistics.
7 What's the difference between batch norm, layer norm, and instance norm?
Batch norm normalizes across the batch dimension (per feature), depends on batch size. Layer norm normalizes across features within a single sample (batch-independent, used in transformers/RNNs). Instance norm normalizes per-channel per-sample (common in style transfer).
8 Explain the vanishing/exploding gradient problem in deep networks.
During backprop, gradients are products of many terms. If weights/derivatives are <1, gradients vanish toward zero (early layers stop learning); if >1, they explode. Solutions: ReLU, residual connections, batch norm, careful initialization, gradient clipping (for exploding), and LSTM/GRU gating (for RNNs).
12 What are residual connections (ResNets) and why do they help?
Residual connections add the input of a block to its output (skip connection): y = F(x) + x. They let gradients flow directly through the identity path, mitigating vanishing gradients and enabling very deep networks (100+ layers) by letting layers learn residual functions rather than full mappings.
26 What is gradient clipping and when is it needed?
Gradient clipping caps gradient magnitude (by value or norm) to prevent exploding gradients, especially in RNNs/Transformers. It stabilizes training by avoiding huge, destabilizing weight updates.
3 · CNNs

🖼️ Convolutional Networks

9 How do CNNs work and why are they good for images?
CNNs use convolutional filters that slide over the input to detect local patterns, sharing weights across spatial positions. This gives parameter efficiency, translation invariance, and a hierarchy of features (edges → textures → objects). Pooling layers reduce spatial size and add invariance.
10 What are the roles of stride, padding, and pooling in CNNs?
Stride controls the step size of the filter (larger stride = smaller output). Padding adds border pixels to preserve spatial dimensions ("same" padding) or allow shrinkage ("valid"). Pooling (max/average) downsamples feature maps, reducing computation and providing local translation invariance.
11 How do you compute the output size of a convolutional layer?
Output = ⌊(W − F + 2P) / S⌋ + 1, where W is input size, F is filter size, P is padding, and S is stride. Applied independently to height and width.
4 · RNNs, Attention & Transformers

🔗 Sequence Models

13 Explain how RNNs work and their limitations.
RNNs process sequences by maintaining a hidden state updated at each timestep, sharing weights across time. Limitations: vanishing/exploding gradients over long sequences (poor long-term memory), sequential computation (no parallelization), and difficulty capturing long-range dependencies.
14 How do LSTMs solve the vanishing gradient problem?
LSTMs use a cell state with gating mechanisms (forget, input, output gates) that regulate information flow. The cell state allows gradients to flow nearly unchanged across many timesteps (additive updates instead of repeated multiplication), preserving long-term dependencies. GRUs are a simpler variant with fewer gates.
15 Explain the attention mechanism.
Attention lets a model weigh the relevance of different input parts when producing each output. It computes attention weights via compatibility between a query and keys, then takes a weighted sum of values: Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QKᵀ / √dₖ) · V. The scaling by √dₖ prevents large dot products from saturating softmax.
16 How do Transformers work and why did they replace RNNs?
Transformers use self-attention to relate all positions in a sequence simultaneously, plus positional encodings (since attention is order-agnostic), multi-head attention, feed-forward layers, and residual connections + layer norm. They enable full parallelization, capture long-range dependencies better, and scale far more effectively than sequential RNNs.
17 What is multi-head attention and why use multiple heads?
Multi-head attention runs several attention operations in parallel with different learned projections, letting the model attend to different representation subspaces and relationship types simultaneously (e.g., syntactic vs. semantic). Outputs are concatenated and projected.
18 Why do Transformers need positional encoding?
Self-attention is permutation-invariant and has no inherent notion of order. Positional encodings (sinusoidal or learned) inject sequence position information into embeddings so the model can use word order.
30 Explain encoder-decoder architecture.
An encoder compresses the input into a latent representation; a decoder generates output from it. Used in seq2seq tasks (translation, summarization). Transformers use stacked encoder/decoder blocks with cross-attention letting the decoder attend to encoder outputs.
5 · Regularization & Generalization

🛡️ Fighting Overfitting

19 What is the difference between dropout in training vs inference?
During training, dropout randomly zeroes neurons to prevent co-adaptation. At inference, all neurons are active. With inverted dropout, activations are scaled by 1 / (1 − p) during training so no scaling is needed at inference, keeping expected activations consistent.
21 What is transfer learning and when do you use it?
Transfer learning reuses a model pretrained on a large dataset (e.g., ImageNet, large text corpora) for a new related task. You either use it as a fixed feature extractor or fine-tune some/all layers. Useful when target data is limited, saving compute and improving generalization.
22 What's the difference between fine-tuning and feature extraction in transfer learning?
Feature extraction freezes the pretrained layers and trains only a new head on top — fast, low data needs. Fine-tuning unfreezes some or all pretrained layers and updates them with a small learning rate — better performance when you have more data and the domains differ.
23 How do you handle overfitting in deep networks?
Regularization (L1/L2, dropout), data augmentation, early stopping, batch normalization, reducing model size, transfer learning, and gathering more data. Monitoring the validation loss gap signals overfitting.
24 What is data augmentation and why is it useful?
Data augmentation creates modified copies of training data (rotations, flips, crops, color jitter for images; synonym replacement, back-translation for text). It increases effective dataset size and diversity, improving generalization and robustness without collecting new data.
6 · Outputs & Optimization

🎯 Heads & Optimizers

25 Explain the difference between softmax and sigmoid for output layers.
Sigmoid outputs independent probabilities per class — used for binary or multi-label classification. Softmax outputs a probability distribution summing to 1 across mutually exclusive classes — used for multi-class single-label classification.
27 Compare Adam, SGD with momentum, and RMSprop for deep learning.
SGD+momentum generalizes well and is common for final-tuning but needs careful learning-rate scheduling. RMSprop adapts per-parameter learning rates via squared-gradient averages, good for non-stationary objectives. Adam combines momentum + adaptive rates with bias correction — robust default, though sometimes generalizes slightly worse than tuned SGD.
28 What is the role of the learning rate schedule (warmup, decay)?
Warmup gradually increases the learning rate early to avoid instability when weights are random (critical for Transformers). Decay (step, cosine, exponential) reduces it over time for finer convergence near the minimum. Together they improve stability and final performance.
