Interview Questions · Deep Dives

Advanced Follow-Up Questions & Answers

Twenty senior-level follow-ups across ML, deep learning, and LLM systems — the "why" and "what would you do" questions that interviewers ask after the textbook answer, covering math, debugging, and production tradeoffs.

1 · Architectures & Attention Math

🧱 Why Models Are Built the Way They Are

1 What's the difference between BERT and GPT pretraining objectives?
BERT uses Masked Language Modeling (MLM) — randomly masks ~15% of tokens and predicts them using bidirectional context (sees both left and right), plus originally Next Sentence Prediction. GPT uses Causal Language Modeling (CLM) — predicts the next token using only left context (autoregressive). BERT's bidirectionality suits understanding tasks; GPT's left-to-right objective enables generation.
7 Why does deeper not always mean better in neural networks?
Beyond a point, added depth brings vanishing/exploding gradients, optimization difficulty, overfitting, diminishing returns, and higher compute/latency. Without architectural aids (residual connections, normalization), very deep networks train poorly. Capacity must also match data size — too deep on limited data overfits. ResNets specifically addressed degradation where deeper plain networks had higher training error.
17 Why does attention use scaling by √dₖ, and what happens without it?
As dimension dₖ grows, dot products Q · K have larger magnitude (variance scales with dₖ), pushing softmax into saturated regions where gradients vanish and attention becomes nearly one-hot. Dividing by √dₖ normalizes the variance back to ~1, keeping softmax in a sensitive range with healthy gradients. Without scaling, training becomes unstable and slow for large dₖ.
2 · Training Stability & Diagnostics

🩺 Debugging Networks in the Wild

8 What causes loss to become NaN during training and how do you debug it?
Common causes: learning rate too high (exploding gradients), numerical instability (log(0), division by zero), bad input data (NaNs/infs), exploding activations, or mixed-precision overflow. Debug by lowering the learning rate, adding gradient clipping, checking/normalizing inputs, using numerically stable ops (log-sum-exp, epsilon in denominators), reducing batch size, and using loss scaling for FP16.
9 Why is training loss decreasing but validation loss increasing?
This is the classic overfitting signature — the model memorizes training data and fails to generalize. Remedies: regularization (dropout, weight decay), data augmentation, early stopping at the validation minimum, reducing model capacity, gathering more data, or transfer learning. Also verify train/validation come from the same distribution and there's no data leakage.
10 How does BatchNorm behave with batch size = 1, and what do you use instead?
With batch size 1, batch statistics (mean/variance) are unreliable — variance is zero or meaningless — destabilizing training. BatchNorm depends on sufficient batch size for good estimates. Alternatives that are batch-independent: LayerNorm (normalizes across features per sample, used in transformers), GroupNorm, or InstanceNorm — all work fine with tiny or single-sample batches.
14 Walk through the math of backpropagation for a single layer.
For a layer with z = Wx + b, a = σ(z), and loss L: the upstream gradient ∂L/∂a arrives from the next layer. Then ∂L/∂z = ∂L/∂a · σ'(z) (elementwise). Weight gradient ∂L/∂W = ∂L/∂z · xᵀ, bias gradient ∂L/∂b = ∂L/∂z, and the gradient passed back to the previous layer ∂L/∂x = Wᵀ · ∂L/∂z. The chain rule composes these across layers from output to input.
20 Why might tuned SGD generalize better than Adam, despite Adam converging faster?
Adam's adaptive per-parameter learning rates can converge to sharper minima and overfit, sometimes generalizing slightly worse. SGD (with momentum) tends to find flatter minima associated with better generalization, and its noisier updates act as implicit regularization — but it needs careful learning-rate scheduling. A common strategy: train with Adam early for speed, then switch to SGD, or use AdamW (decoupled weight decay) which closes much of the gap.
3 · Classical ML Deep Dives

📐 Edge Cases in Familiar Algorithms

11 What happens to logistic regression when classes are perfectly separable?
With perfect separation, the maximum-likelihood weights diverge toward infinity as the model pushes predicted probabilities to exactly 0 and 1, so training doesn't converge and coefficients blow up. Fixes: add regularization (L1/L2) to bound the weights, use early stopping, or apply Bayesian priors. This is why regularization is often essential, not optional.
12 How does the choice of k in KNN affect bias and variance?
Small k (e.g., k=1) gives low bias but high variance — sensitive to noise, jagged decision boundary, overfitting. Large k gives high bias but low variance — smoother boundary, may underfit and blur class distinctions. k controls the bias-variance tradeoff; it's tuned via cross-validation, and odd k avoids ties in binary classification.
13 Why is accuracy a poor metric for imbalanced datasets?
With heavy imbalance (e.g., 99% negatives), a model predicting only the majority class achieves 99% accuracy while being useless for the minority class. Accuracy hides poor minority-class performance. Better metrics: precision, recall, F1, ROC-AUC, and especially PR-AUC, plus the confusion matrix — these reveal performance on the rare but often important class.
4 · RLHF, DPO & Reward Hacking

🎚️ Alignment in Practice

3 Why is RLHF (PPO) unstable, and how does DPO fix it?
PPO-based RLHF involves a separate reward model that can be hacked (reward hacking), needs careful KL-penalty tuning to prevent the policy drifting too far, and RL training is sensitive to hyperparameters with high variance. DPO eliminates the reward model and RL loop entirely, deriving a closed-form loss that directly optimizes the policy on preference pairs using a simple classification-style objective — making it stable, reproducible, and cheaper.
15 What is reward hacking in RLHF and how do you mitigate it?
Reward hacking is when the policy exploits flaws in the reward model to get high reward without genuinely improving (e.g., overly verbose answers, sycophancy, gaming length). Mitigations: KL-divergence penalty keeping the policy near the SFT model, better/more diverse reward-model training data, reward-model ensembles, periodic reward-model retraining, and methods like DPO that avoid a separate exploitable reward model.
5 · LLM Inference & Serving

⚡ Production Throughput & Cost

2 How does FlashAttention save memory and speed up attention?
Standard attention materializes the full n × n attention matrix in slow GPU memory (HBM), causing O(n²) memory and many memory reads/writes. FlashAttention uses tiling and online softmax to compute attention in blocks within fast on-chip SRAM, never materializing the full matrix. It's exact (not an approximation) and IO-aware, reducing memory to O(n) and dramatically speeding up by minimizing HBM traffic.
5 How would you reduce LLM inference cost in production?
Multiple levers: quantization (INT8/INT4) to shrink memory and speed compute; KV caching to avoid recomputation; batching requests (continuous/dynamic batching like vLLM); model distillation or using smaller models for easy queries (model routing/cascades); prompt compression and caching frequent prompts; speculative decoding (small draft model proposes tokens, large model verifies); and infrastructure choices (efficient serving frameworks, autoscaling, spot instances).
6 What is speculative decoding and how does it speed up generation?
A small, fast "draft" model generates several candidate tokens ahead, then the large target model verifies them all in a single parallel forward pass. Accepted tokens are kept; the first rejection triggers correction. Since verification is parallel and the draft is cheap, multiple tokens can be produced per large-model pass — speeding generation 2–3x with identical output distribution (lossless).
18 What's the difference between continuous batching and static batching in LLM serving?
Static batching groups a fixed set of requests and waits for all to finish — the longest sequence stalls the batch (head-of-line blocking), wasting GPU. Continuous (in-flight) batching, used by vLLM, dynamically adds new requests and evicts finished ones at the token level, keeping the GPU saturated. Combined with PagedAttention for efficient KV-cache memory, it massively improves throughput.
6 · RAG, Decoding & Practical Pitfalls

🛠️ Tradeoffs You'll Actually Hit

4 What chunking strategy works best for RAG?
There's no universal best — it depends on data and queries. Common approaches: fixed-size chunks with overlap (simple, preserves context across boundaries), semantic/recursive chunking (split on natural boundaries like paragraphs/sections), and sentence-window or parent-document retrieval (retrieve small precise chunks but feed larger context to the LLM). Key tradeoffs: small chunks improve retrieval precision but lose context; large chunks preserve context but dilute relevance and waste tokens. Adding metadata and overlap (~10–20%) generally helps.
16 How does temperature interact with top-p sampling, and how do you tune them together?
Temperature reshapes the probability distribution (sharper or flatter) before top-p truncation selects the nucleus. High temperature + high top-p maximizes diversity/creativity but risks incoherence; low temperature + low top-p yields focused, deterministic output. Typically you tune one primarily — e.g., temperature ~0.7 with top-p ~0.9 for balanced generation, near-0 temperature for factual/deterministic tasks. Setting both very high compounds randomness.
19 How do you prevent and detect data leakage in a temporal/time-series setting?
Use strictly time-ordered splits (train on past, validate/test on future) — never random shuffling. Avoid features computed using future information (e.g., target leakage, look-ahead bias in aggregations). Fit all preprocessing (scaling, encoding) only on training data. Detect via implausibly high performance, feature-importance audits revealing suspicious predictors, and backtesting that mimics real deployment timing.
