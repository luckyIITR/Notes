Interview Questions · Machine Learning

Advanced ML Interview Questions & Answers

Twenty-five deeper machine-learning questions covering kernels, boosting internals, optimization, generalization theory, and the practical pitfalls that come up in senior-level interviews.

1 · Models & Kernels

🧠 Algorithms in Depth

1 Explain how Support Vector Machines work and the kernel trick.
SVM finds the hyperplane that maximizes the margin between classes (the distance to the nearest points, called support vectors). The kernel trick implicitly maps data into a higher-dimensional space using a kernel function (e.g., RBF, polynomial) without computing the transformation explicitly, enabling non-linear separation. It relies on computing dot products via K(x, x') instead of explicit feature maps.
2 How does XGBoost differ from standard Gradient Boosting?
XGBoost adds regularization (L1/L2) to the objective, uses second-order Taylor approximation (gradients + Hessians) for optimization, supports parallelized tree construction, handles missing values natively, includes column subsampling, and uses a sparsity-aware split-finding algorithm. These make it faster and less prone to overfitting.
3 What is the difference between Gradient Boosting and AdaBoost?
AdaBoost reweights misclassified samples and combines weak learners via weighted voting. Gradient Boosting fits each new learner to the residual gradients of the loss function, optimizing an arbitrary differentiable loss via gradient descent in function space.
7 Explain the difference between generative and discriminative models with examples and tradeoffs.
Generative (Naive Bayes, GMMs) model P(x, y) and can generate samples and handle missing data, but make stronger distributional assumptions. Discriminative (logistic regression, SVM) model P(y | x) directly, usually achieving higher accuracy when data is abundant since they focus only on the decision boundary.
16 How does the Naive Bayes "naive" assumption work, and why does it still perform well?
It assumes features are conditionally independent given the class. Though rarely true, it works because correct classification only requires the correct class having the highest posterior — not accurate probability estimates — so dependency errors often cancel out.
22 Explain the difference between hard and soft margin SVM.
Hard margin requires perfect linear separation (fails with noise/overlap). Soft margin introduces slack variables and a penalty parameter C, allowing some misclassifications to balance margin width and training errors — higher C means less tolerance for errors.
2 · Optimization & Training Dynamics

⚙️ Gradients, Losses & Optimizers

4 Explain the vanishing and exploding gradient problem.
In deep networks, gradients computed via backpropagation can shrink toward zero (vanishing) or grow uncontrollably (exploding) through repeated multiplication of small/large weights. Solutions: ReLU activations, proper weight initialization (Xavier/He), batch normalization, residual connections, and gradient clipping.
5 Why use cross-entropy loss instead of MSE for classification?
Cross-entropy penalizes confident wrong predictions heavily and produces well-behaved (convex with sigmoid/softmax) gradients, leading to faster convergence. MSE with sigmoid creates non-convex loss surfaces and small gradients when predictions are very wrong, slowing learning.
8 How does batch normalization work and why does it help?
It normalizes layer inputs to zero mean and unit variance per mini-batch, then applies learnable scale (γ) and shift (β). It reduces internal covariate shift, allows higher learning rates, acts as a mild regularizer, and smooths the loss landscape for faster, more stable training.
10 Explain how dropout works and why it acts as regularization.
During training, dropout randomly zeroes a fraction of neurons each forward pass, preventing co-adaptation. It approximates training an ensemble of subnetworks; at inference, all neurons are used with scaled weights (or activations scaled during training via inverted dropout).
19 Explain how a learning rate affects training and strategies to tune it.
Too high causes divergence/oscillation; too low causes slow convergence or getting stuck. Strategies: learning rate schedules (step decay, cosine annealing), warmup, adaptive optimizers (Adam, RMSprop), and learning-rate finders.
20 What is the difference between Adam, RMSprop, and SGD with momentum?
Momentum accelerates SGD by accumulating a velocity term in consistent gradient directions. RMSprop scales the learning rate per-parameter by a running average of squared gradients. Adam combines both (momentum + adaptive scaling) with bias correction, making it a robust default.
3 · Theory & Generalization

📐 Math, Priors & Decomposition

6 What is the bias-variance decomposition mathematically?
For squared error: E[(y − ŷ)²] = Bias(ŷ)² + Var(ŷ) + σ². Bias is (E[ŷ] − true value), variance is the spread of ŷ across datasets, and σ² is irreducible noise.
9 What is the difference between L1/L2 regularization from a Bayesian perspective?
L2 regularization corresponds to a Gaussian prior on weights (MAP estimation), L1 corresponds to a Laplace prior. The prior's shape explains why L1 induces sparsity (peaked at zero with heavier tails) while L2 shrinks smoothly.
11 What is the difference between bagging and boosting in terms of bias/variance?
Bagging primarily reduces variance by averaging independent high-variance models. Boosting primarily reduces bias by sequentially fitting models to residual errors, though it can increase variance/overfitting if not regularized.
13 What is the EM algorithm?
Expectation-Maximization iteratively finds maximum likelihood estimates with latent variables. E-step: compute expected values of latent variables given current parameters. M-step: maximize the expected log-likelihood to update parameters. Used in Gaussian Mixture Models; guaranteed to not decrease likelihood.
24 Explain the difference between Type I and Type II errors in ML context.
Type I (false positive) is predicting positive when actually negative. Type II (false negative) is predicting negative when actually positive. The tradeoff depends on cost: medical screening prioritizes minimizing Type II; spam filtering may prioritize minimizing Type I.
4 · Evaluation & Data Issues

📊 Metrics, Leakage & Practical Pitfalls

12 How do you choose the number of clusters in K-means?
Elbow method (plot inertia vs. k, find the bend), silhouette score (measures cohesion vs. separation), gap statistic, or domain knowledge. K-means also assumes spherical, equally-sized clusters, which limits applicability.
14 Explain ROC-AUC vs PR-AUC and when to use each.
ROC-AUC plots TPR vs FPR and is robust to threshold choice but can look optimistic on imbalanced data. PR-AUC (precision vs recall) focuses on the positive class and is more informative when positives are rare, since it ignores true negatives.
15 What is the difference between feature selection and feature extraction?
Feature selection picks a subset of original features (filter, wrapper, embedded methods). Feature extraction creates new features by transforming the original space (PCA, autoencoders, LDA). Selection preserves interpretability; extraction can capture richer combined signals.
17 Explain the difference between collaborative filtering and content-based filtering.
Collaborative filtering recommends based on user-item interaction patterns (users similar to you liked X). Content-based filtering uses item/user feature similarity (you liked items with these attributes). Collaborative suffers from cold-start; content-based may over-specialize.
18 What is multicollinearity and why is it a problem?
When predictors are highly correlated, it inflates coefficient variance, making estimates unstable and hard to interpret in linear models (though prediction may be unaffected). Detected via VIF; addressed with regularization, PCA, or dropping correlated features.
21 How do you detect and handle data leakage?
Data leakage is when training data includes information unavailable at prediction time, inflating performance. Detect via suspiciously high accuracy or features too predictive. Prevent by proper train/test splitting before preprocessing, time-based splits for temporal data, and fitting scalers/encoders only on training data.
23 What is the difference between online and batch learning?
Batch learning trains on the entire dataset at once and retrains for updates. Online learning updates incrementally as new data arrives, suiting streaming data and concept drift, with lower memory needs but more sensitivity to noisy samples.
25 How does feature scaling affect different algorithms?
Distance-based (KNN, K-means, SVM) and gradient-based (neural nets, logistic regression) algorithms require scaling for fair feature contribution and stable convergence. Tree-based models (decision trees, Random Forest, XGBoost) are scale-invariant and don't need it.
