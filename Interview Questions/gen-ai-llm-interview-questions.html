Interview Questions · Gen AI · LLMs

Gen AI / LLM Interview Questions & Answers

Thirty questions on the modern LLM stack — transformer internals, training stages, RLHF/DPO, RAG, PEFT/LoRA, decoding, scaling laws, quantization, agents, and the safety issues that come with them.

1 · Transformer Foundations

🧱 Architectures & Attention

1 What is a Large Language Model and how is it trained?
An LLM is a transformer-based neural network trained on massive text corpora to predict the next token given context. Training has stages: pretraining (self-supervised next-token prediction on large unlabeled text), supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on instruction-response pairs, and alignment (e.g., RLHF) to make outputs helpful and safe.
2 Explain the self-attention mechanism mathematically.
Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(QKᵀ / √dₖ) · V. Each token is projected into Query, Key, and Value vectors. Dot products of Q and K measure relevance between tokens, scaled by √dₖ to stabilize gradients, passed through softmax to get attention weights, then used to take a weighted sum of Values. This lets each token gather context from all others.
3 What's the difference between encoder-only, decoder-only, and encoder-decoder models?
Encoder-only (BERT) uses bidirectional attention, good for understanding tasks (classification, NER). Decoder-only (GPT) uses causal/masked attention, good for generation. Encoder-decoder (T5, BART) encodes input bidirectionally then generates output, good for seq2seq tasks like translation and summarization.
4 What is causal (masked) attention and why do decoder models use it?
Causal attention masks future tokens so each position can only attend to previous tokens. Decoder models use it during autoregressive generation to prevent "cheating" by seeing the answer — preserving the next-token prediction objective and enabling left-to-right generation.
5 Explain tokenization and the role of BPE.
Tokenization splits text into units (tokens) the model processes. Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) iteratively merges the most frequent character/subword pairs, balancing vocabulary size and coverage. Subword tokenization handles rare/unknown words by breaking them into known pieces, avoiding out-of-vocabulary issues. Variants: WordPiece (BERT), SentencePiece, tiktoken.
6 What are embeddings and how do they capture meaning?
Embeddings are dense vector representations of tokens/text where semantic similarity corresponds to geometric proximity. They're learned so words/sentences with similar meanings have nearby vectors, enabling similarity search, clustering, and downstream tasks. Contextual embeddings (from transformers) vary by surrounding context.
2 · Training, Alignment & Fine-Tuning

🎚️ From Pretraining to Preferences

7 What is RLHF and why is it important?
Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback aligns models with human preferences. Steps: (1) SFT on demonstrations, (2) train a reward model on human-ranked outputs, (3) optimize the LLM against the reward model using RL (typically PPO). It improves helpfulness, harmlessness, and instruction-following beyond what next-token prediction alone achieves.
8 What is DPO and how does it differ from RLHF/PPO?
Direct Preference Optimization aligns models directly from preference data without training a separate reward model or running RL. It reframes preference optimization as a simple classification-style loss on the policy itself, making it more stable, simpler, and computationally cheaper than PPO-based RLHF while achieving comparable alignment.
12 What is the difference between fine-tuning and prompt engineering?
Prompt engineering shapes behavior via input instructions without changing weights — fast, cheap, no training. Fine-tuning updates model weights on task-specific data — better for consistent specialized behavior, domain adaptation, or style, but requires data, compute, and risks catastrophic forgetting.
13 Explain LoRA and why it's efficient.
Low-Rank Adaptation freezes the pretrained weights and injects small trainable low-rank matrices (A and B, where ΔW = BA) into layers. Only these tiny matrices are trained, drastically reducing trainable parameters and memory while preserving the base model. Adapters can be swapped per task. QLoRA adds quantization for even lower memory.
14 What is PEFT (Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning)?
PEFT methods adapt LLMs by training only a small fraction of parameters. Techniques: LoRA/QLoRA (low-rank adapters), prefix/prompt tuning (learnable virtual tokens), and adapter layers. They cut compute/storage costs, reduce catastrophic forgetting, and let one base model serve many tasks via swappable modules.
24 What is catastrophic forgetting and how do you prevent it?
Catastrophic forgetting is when fine-tuning on new tasks degrades performance on previously learned ones. Prevention: PEFT/LoRA (preserve base weights), low learning rates, rehearsal (mixing in original data), regularization (EWC), and multi-task training.
26 What is the difference between pretraining, fine-tuning, and alignment?
Pretraining teaches general language via self-supervised next-token prediction on huge corpora. Fine-tuning (SFT) adapts the model to follow instructions or specialize using labeled data. Alignment (RLHF/DPO) tunes outputs to match human preferences for helpfulness and safety. Each stage builds on the previous.
3 · RAG & Embeddings

📚 Grounding LLMs in External Knowledge

9 Explain RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation).
RAG combines a retriever with a generator: relevant documents are retrieved (usually via vector similarity search) from an external knowledge base and added to the prompt as context. This grounds responses in up-to-date/proprietary data, reduces hallucinations, and avoids retraining the model for new knowledge.
10 What are the components of a RAG pipeline?
(1) Document ingestion and chunking, (2) embedding generation, (3) vector store/index, (4) retriever (similarity search, often with reranking), (5) prompt augmentation with retrieved context, (6) the LLM generator. Quality depends heavily on chunking strategy, embedding quality, and retrieval relevance.
29 What are embeddings vs generative outputs — when do you use each?
Embedding models produce fixed vectors for similarity, search, clustering, and retrieval (no text generation). Generative models produce text. RAG uses both: embeddings for retrieval, generation for the final answer. Choose embeddings for semantic search/classification, generation for content creation.
4 · Prompting & Decoding

💬 Steering Outputs at Inference

11 Why do LLMs hallucinate and how do you reduce hallucinations?
LLMs predict plausible tokens, not verified facts — they have no inherent truth-checking and may confidently generate false information, especially for rare or out-of-distribution queries. Mitigations: RAG grounding, citing sources, lower temperature, prompting for "I don't know," fine-tuning, self-consistency checks, and constrained decoding.
15 Explain temperature, top-k, and top-p (nucleus) sampling.
Temperature scales logits before softmax — lower (<1) makes output more deterministic/focused, higher (>1) more random/creative. Top-k restricts sampling to the k most likely tokens. Top-p (nucleus) samples from the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability exceeds p, adapting the candidate pool dynamically.
16 What is the difference between greedy decoding and beam search?
Greedy decoding picks the single highest-probability token at each step — fast but can miss globally better sequences. Beam search keeps the top-b candidate sequences at each step, exploring more paths for higher overall likelihood — better for tasks like translation but more compute and can produce generic/repetitive text.
17 What is in-context learning and few-shot prompting?
In-context learning is the model's ability to perform tasks from examples in the prompt without weight updates. Zero-shot gives only instructions; few-shot provides several input-output examples that guide the model to infer the pattern and format. Performance often improves with well-chosen examples.
18 Explain Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting.
CoT prompts the model to generate intermediate reasoning steps before the final answer (e.g., "Let's think step by step"). It improves performance on multi-step reasoning (math, logic) by allocating more computation and structuring the problem. Variants: zero-shot CoT, self-consistency (sample multiple chains, majority vote), tree-of-thoughts.
5 · Efficiency, Scaling & Inference

⚡ Making Big Models Practical

19 What is the context window and why does it matter?
The context window is the maximum number of tokens (input + output) a model can process at once. It limits how much information/history fits in a prompt. Longer windows enable processing large documents and longer conversations but increase compute cost (attention scales quadratically with sequence length).
20 Why does attention scale quadratically, and how is this addressed?
Self-attention compares every token with every other token → O(n²) compute and memory in sequence length. Solutions: FlashAttention (memory-efficient exact attention), sparse/local attention, sliding-window attention, linear attention approximations, and architectures like Longformer/Mamba (state-space models) for long sequences.
21 What is KV caching and why is it important for inference?
During autoregressive generation, the Key and Value vectors of past tokens are recomputed each step. KV caching stores them so only the new token's K/V are computed, drastically speeding up generation. It trades memory for speed and is essential for efficient LLM serving.
22 Explain quantization and its tradeoffs.
Quantization reduces weight/activation precision (e.g., FP16 → INT8/INT4), shrinking memory footprint and speeding inference. Tradeoff: potential accuracy loss. Methods: post-training quantization (PTQ, fast, may degrade) and quantization-aware training (QAT, better accuracy, costlier). Techniques like GPTQ and AWQ minimize quality loss at low bit-widths.
23 What are scaling laws for LLMs?
Scaling laws describe how model performance (loss) improves predictably as a power law with increased model size, dataset size, and compute. The Chinchilla finding showed many models were undertrained — optimal performance balances parameters and training tokens (roughly 20 tokens per parameter), guiding compute-efficient training.
27 What are mixture-of-experts (MoE) models?
MoE replaces dense feed-forward layers with multiple "expert" subnetworks and a router that activates only a few experts per token (sparse activation). This increases total parameters/capacity while keeping per-token compute low, improving efficiency. Challenges: load balancing across experts and training stability.
6 · Evaluation, Safety & Agents

🛡️ Deploying LLMs in the Real World

25 How do you evaluate LLMs?
Automated benchmarks (MMLU, HellaSwag, GSM8K, HumanEval for code), task metrics (BLEU, ROUGE, perplexity), LLM-as-a-judge (using a strong model to score outputs), human evaluation, and red-teaming for safety. No single metric suffices; alignment and factuality often need human/preference-based assessment.
28 What is prompt injection and how do you defend against it?
Prompt injection is an attack where malicious input overrides the system's intended instructions (e.g., "ignore previous instructions"). Defenses: input/output filtering, separating system and user content, instruction hierarchies, sandboxing tool use, and not feeding untrusted content directly into privileged prompts. It remains an open security challenge, especially with RAG and agents.
30 Explain agents and tool use in LLMs.
Agentic LLMs go beyond text generation by planning and invoking external tools (search, code execution, APIs, databases) in a loop, observing results and deciding next actions. Frameworks use reasoning patterns like ReAct (reason + act). This extends capabilities to real-time data and actions but adds reliability, latency, and safety challenges.
